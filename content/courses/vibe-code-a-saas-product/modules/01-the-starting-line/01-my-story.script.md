My wife Becca and I built Dubsado. It's a business management platform for creative professionals — freelancers, photographers, coaches, designers — people running businesses who needed real tools to manage their clients, contracts, invoices, and workflows.

Today it's an 8-figure ARR company serving over 30,000 small businesses, and we never took a dollar of investor money.

But the version we launched with was nothing like that. The first Dubsado could have been vibe coded in an hour. It barely did anything. It did one thing well enough that a specific group of people would pay for it — and that was enough to start.

How did we build it? Becca handled marketing. I built the product. That was the team. We didn't have a pitch deck. There were times I wasn't even sure what we were building. But we had a set of problems we understood deeply, because both of us were running creative businesses and living the pain every day.

We built customers one at a time. Becca would get on calls, walk people through the product, listen to what they needed, and I'd go build it. Sometimes that same day.

There was no big launch. We started an Instagram and collected 250 emails before the app was ready to show anyone. No Product Hunt moment. Growth was slow on purpose. One customer became ten. Ten became a hundred. A hundred became a thousand. And it kept going.

People ask us all the time why we didn't raise money. We wanted our users to be our biggest stakeholders. When you take investor money, you answer to investors. When your customers are your investors, you answer to them. That shapes everything — what you build, what you prioritize, how you treat the people paying you.

Everyone's SaaS goals are different. You might want — or need — to take outside investment, and that's fine. For us, it was never about growing fast or chasing a payday. We wanted something sustainable that served real people and gave us the freedom to live the life we wanted.

Building a SaaS is hard. It's stressful. Some days everything breaks and you're the only one who can fix it. Some nights you can't stop thinking about the thing that's not working.

It's also fun. Deeply rewarding. You're your own boss. You're building something that matters to people. And every so often you watch someone use what you made and it actually helps them. That never gets old.

After I built up a team to take over development, I stepped back. No flashy acquisition. Just a company that runs without me — which was the goal all along.

Since then, I've been helping female founders build, fix, and scale the tech behind their SaaS products — shipping more to their audiences, generating more revenue, running bigger launches. The same patterns show up every time: smart people with great ideas who just need someone to show them the path from "I have an idea" to "I have a product."

That's what this course is.
